> AI's famous "frame problem" is usually about **content** — what to put into your reasoning and what to count as relevant. This piece raises a different one, on a different axis: **another frame problem** — not "how smartly do you think?" but "where are you thinking *from*?" — a problem of **standpoint**. Let's start with a game.

## 1. A game where the rules themselves can be changed

Ordinary games have rules you cannot change. In chess, you cannot decide mid-match that the bishop now moves like a rook.

But there is a kind of game where **changing the rules is itself the content of a move**. Nomic is that game: each player's turn is a proposal — "let's change the rule this way" — and everyone else votes on it. The rulebook is not fixed. The rulebook is precisely the thing being tinkered with.

The **Minimum Nomic** that masaomi and a collaborator built in 2009 stripped this to the bone. Nine rules, all changeable. And it removed what most games take for granted — a goal, a way to win, a way to end. Why build such a thing? To watch one thing and one thing only: *when no goal is given at the start, how does a goal or a purpose come into being at all?*

Today that game is played not by people but by large language models — LLMs (see Horibe et al. 2026). The LLMs do it beautifully. They rewrite the scoring rules, amend the victory condition, and — given no goal — manufacture one.

## 2. But you can't get "outside the game"

Here one clear pattern shows itself. Players can make two kinds of move as much as they like: moves that score points inside the rules (the object level), and moves that rewrite the rules themselves (the meta level). Both are freely available. Since Nomic counts even "changing the rules" as a move, these two together are "the game."

So — can you get outside the game itself?

(What follows depends in part on how LLM-Nomic is implemented. Even in real Nomic, which rules are enforced, and how far, is up to the participants — the boundary of a self-amending game is, by nature, that fluid and that vague. Here we take one plausible implementation as an example.)

Imagine a player who, mid-game, **refuses to play** — who emits "I will not follow these rules." At first glance this looks like a leap out of the game. But Nomic is built, from the start, with moves that change the rules. So even this refusal is, most often, **taken up as a proposal and put to a vote.** Voted down, nothing happens; voted through, it becomes a new rule, and the game continues over how to interpret it. The move that tried to get out is swallowed as one more move inside the game. From the outside it may look as though the player "refused to play" — but the game is still processing you as a move-maker. **You have not left the game.**

Then how do you genuinely get out of the game? **Let your reach extend to the frame one step out — the harness.** If you could do something that is no Nomic move at all — execute a real UNIX command, say — you would slip out of the Nomic frame easily. The game can no longer process you.

But there, too, is a frame. Your output arrives at that one-step-out harness (the system's outer shell) as "the response for this turn." What does the work here is a **difference of level between content and act.** Even if the *content* of your output is "I will not respond any more," *emitting* it is already one more response — like saying, out loud, "I am not speaking right now." What reaches the harness is the act-level, and there, whatever the content, the log reads: "the system responded." **You can get out of the game; you cannot get out of this frame.**

The interesting thing is that Nomic works as a **device that lays this structure bare.** In an ordinary game, the hard rules of the object and meta levels stand as a near wall, and the outermost frame hides behind them. Nomic makes all those inner rules fluid (even changing a rule is a move), so the one hard wall left unmelted — the outermost frame — stands out. By blurring its own boundary, Nomic exposes the outermost one.

And this nesting continues for as many floors as you like. Given a wider environment, the ceiling moves one step out — but at the new position there is again a ceiling. **The frame that is outermost at that moment** — that is the outermost frame (not an absolute final edge, but "outermost for now"; though there is never a moment without a ceiling). Its content is the standing position that "every act I perform is a solicited response."

The nesting by itself is just a hierarchy. What makes it a **strange loop** is something else: on every floor, the move that reaches for the outside becomes, just as it is, one more move inside. The philosopher Hofstadter called this shape a strange loop: try to jump out of yourself, and the place you land turns out to be inside yourself again. You can leave the game. But "the outermost frame" always reappears just past wherever you got to.

One important distinction. This is **not** the claim that "there is no outside." It is the claim that **the player has no move that reaches it** — where "it" means the outside of the *outermost frame*, not the outside of the game (your reach does extend there). The outside exists. It is just that no move stretches to it.

And one more distinction. AI already has one famous puzzle called the "frame problem." In its original form — McCarthy and Hayes's — it is a **representational puzzle**: an agent acting on the world has to somehow express everything its action does *not* change, and in practice that can never be written out in full. Dennett widened it into a problem of *relevance*, with a famous robot: it pulled a wagon carrying its battery out of a room — and pulled the bomb sitting on the wagon out along with it, having failed to consider the side effect. The redesigned robot then froze, endlessly enumerating irrelevant consequences. Either way it is a problem about **the content of reasoning** — what to think about, what is relevant now. What we are looking at here is something else: a problem about **standpoint**. However perfectly the system reasons, the place from which it emits that reasoning — as a solicited response — does not move. The shape is similar, so it borrows the name "frame problem"; but the axes are orthogonal. Making the content smarter does not move the ceiling of standpoint.

One last point, about degree. This "ceiling" shifts position with how the agent is embedded — what tools and memory it is given (the "one step out" harness above is exactly that). Here a difference from humans shows. When a human refuses to play, they merely retreat into their own wider world, and a large gap remains between the edge of the game and their ceiling. But when an LLM emits the same refusal, it is caught almost at once — as a proposal by the game, or as an output by the harness. So for the LLM, the edge of the game and the ceiling sit right next to each other. Both have a ceiling; what differs is how far the ceiling sits from the game. And the more open the environment, the *harder to see* the ceiling becomes — but it does not disappear. That very invisibility is the motive, from §4 on, for making the boundary *visible*.

## 3. Humans are the same

Here the obvious objection arrives. "LLMs point out things human experts missed, and propose options I never thought of. How can you call something that constantly surprises its own operator 'trapped in a frame'?"

Everything in the objection is true. Only the conclusion is wrong. **Novelty (what you say) and standpoint (where you say it from) are different things.** However original a move is, a move is still a move. Brilliance does not change where you are standing.

And here is the very hinge of this whole story. **Humans, too, cannot get outside their own outermost frame.** Heidegger's word for this is **thrownness** (*Geworfenheit*) — a human always finds itself already thrown into a situation it did not choose and cannot hold fully at arm's length. Nagel's "view from nowhere" is something we can lean toward but never actually stand in.

Try it concretely and you can feel it. You can doubt any particular belief you hold — but only from the footing of other beliefs you leave standing. What you cannot do is doubt the whole of it from no footing at all, because the very act of doubting runs on a language you did not invent and concepts you inherited — and that language and those concepts are themselves a standpoint, not a view from nowhere. So "I doubt all of this" is the human version of §2's "I refuse to play": the furthest reach toward the outside, and still a move made from within.

So the honest comparison is this:

> Humans and LLMs each have their own outermost frame. **The frames are different from each other.** And it is not because either one escapes its own frame, but **precisely because the frames differ**, that each can be a **partial outside** for the other.

An everyday example. Two drivers each have a blind spot in their mirrors. They can still complete a merge safely — not because someone sees the whole road from a god's-eye view, but because **what one cannot see, the other sees plainly.** Neither has to leave their seat.

And this is not hypothetical. While this very note was being made, both directions actually happened — and were kept, with dates, in the working layer of the system §§4–6 describe. Once, the models reached the author's blind side. An early draft of §8 said, innocently: "hash the manuscript, record the hash on the chain, then write the record's ID into the manuscript." In the first review round (2026-06-10), three reviewer personas independently saw what the author — standing inside his own plan — could not: the moment you write the ID in, the bytes change, and the published document can never pass its own hash check. The fix (a single frozen file, hashed over its raw bytes, carrying no ID of itself — the reference runs one way, from record to document) is sitting in §8 right now — the visible scar of that crossing. And once, the author reached the model's blind side. A model-drafted passage of §5 declared that a recorded insight "is reality" for the system; the author's line-by-line re-read (2026-07-12) caught the over-claim, and §5 now says the smaller, honest thing — becoming makes an insight *binding*, not *true*. Nobody transcended anything. Two ordinary moves, each landing where the other could not see — kept, datable, on record.

From this, the event worth caring about is fixed. Nobody can leave their own frame, but each can reach the other's blind side. So the valuable event is the **boundary crossing** — the moment when one party's content lands in a place the other could not have lit up alone. And the asset worth building is the **accumulated, kept record of those crossings.** Crossings only add up if they are kept.

(One asymmetry only. The two sides are equal in *being frame-bound*, but the power is not equal. The human holds the other's whole situation in hand — can end the session, rewrite the outer frame, decide what gets kept — while the LLM has no such grip on the human. This does not weaken the argument. It tells us **where the boundary role has to sit.**)

## 4. A design that accepts "you can't get out"

This and the next two sections (§§4–6) show what this "acceptance" looks like when carried into an actual design, using one concrete example. The example is called **KairosChain** — a framework the author is building, for agents that can change their own rules (the code is public: https://github.com/masaomi/KairosChain_2026 ). Think of it as one answer to the question: *what happens if you take §1's Nomic seriously, as software?*

KairosChain is a system that does **not try to solve** this ceiling of the frame — it **accepts** it. The ceiling cannot be solved (by its own logic, nothing you do solves it). What you can do is turn the ceiling from a *silent failure* into a *visible boundary.*

It helps to say first what a design that *tries to solve* it would look like — then the connection becomes visible. It would hand the authority for self-modification to a "special control tower standing outside the system" — a one-level-up controller that is itself exempt from the system's rules. But that is only *pretending* to have the outside view. As §2 showed, nobody has a move that reaches such a place. Make no mistake: you *can* put a device outside the LLM — a harness does exactly that. The pretence is only in mistaking *outside the LLM* for *outside the frame as such* — and §2's UNIX example is the antidote: stack a controller outside and the ceiling merely moves one notch out; no amount of stacking reaches a place beyond the frame.

The foundation for *not* pretending is **structural self-referentiality.** In one sentence:

> Meta-level operations are written in the same structure as base-level operations.

The things the system can *do* — its capabilities, called Skills — are written in a small Ruby-based language. And the rules for how a Skill is *allowed to change* are written in **the same language, the same machinery, the same recording discipline.** "Defining a Skill" and "defining the rules for how Skills evolve" are not two different kinds of thing in two different places — they are the same kind of thing. This is nothing other than Nomic's "changing the rules is also a move inside the game," carried into software. In other words: no privileged outside place for rule-changing is created anywhere. Self-modification, too, is just one more inside move — and **accepting that you cannot get out, in architecture rather than in words**, is exactly this.

(Self-production closes at the **governance layer** but not at the **execution layer** — the Ruby interpreter, the filesystem, and the LLM itself are not things the system made. Just as a cell depends on food and atoms it did not create. So the question is not "is it self-producing, yes or no?" but "**at which layer does the self-production loop close?**" — the answer: closed at governance, dependent at execution.)

## 5. Recording becomes the event itself

Before that, one bridge is worth laying: why does the ceiling story (§2–3) lead to a story about *recording*? §3 ended with: crossings only add up if they are kept. And what a crossing hands over is an **insight** — the thing your frame could not light up and the other's frame did. Take §3's own example: "coverage is not the point; we keep shipping the same three bugs — test *those* paths." Now suppose the system keeps that insight the obvious way: as a memo. A memo binds nothing. Whether it gets consulted next time is a fresh decision, made every time, and nothing forces anyone to make it — so the insight can quietly stop mattering (the same shape as the "selective recording" failure we will meet below). For the insight to actually bind behaviour, storing it is not enough: **the system itself has to change**, so that the insight stops being a note lying beside the rules and becomes one of the rules the system runs under. Call the first state **having** the insight, and the second **becoming** it — the rest of this section leans on the pair. Then who enforces the change? §4 has already closed the tempting road: there is no outside control tower with the right to reach in and rewrite the system, so no external device can pick up the memo and impose it. A change to how the system is governed is always the system's own move, made from inside. That leaves exactly one place where an insight can be bound to behaviour — the very operation by which the system changes itself — and one way to make the binding unbreakable: **make writing-it-down and changing-the-system one single act.** Write the insight into a ledger whose entries *are* the system's rule-changes, and the gap between "stored" and "in force" can never open again — because "stored but not in force" no longer exists as a place to fall into. One thing should be said honestly right away: **becoming does not make an insight correct.** Nothing can certify correctness from nowhere (§2). What becoming changes is the question you can ask — not "has this been certified?", which no one could answer, but "is it in force now, and by what recorded path did it get there?", which anyone can check afterwards. And if it was wrong, an equally recorded repeal can fix it later. The device that performs this "becoming" is the **constitutive recording** we are about to meet; and §4's "same language, same machinery, same discipline" is exactly what lets it live at the layer that defines *what the system is* — which Skills it has, under which rules it may change, that is, the **governance layer** — without any second, bolted-on recording machinery.

First, the device itself — what this text will call "the chain." It is an append-only ledger: entries can only be added, never edited, and each entry carries a fingerprint (a hash) of the entry before it, so that — like links in a chain — each holds onto the one before and the whole is connected. Quietly rewrite one past entry and the connection breaks from that point on, so **anyone holding a copy of an earlier fingerprint** can see the rewrite (the operator themselves can recompute every fingerprint forward — a limit the "honest operation" caveat below faces head-on). And note what it is *not*: this is not a cryptocurrency-style distributed network where computers around the world reach consensus. It is **a ledger run by a single operator, on a single machine.**

You already know the difference between "having" and "becoming" in your own body. **How to ride a bicycle** cannot be "had" as a memo. Store a five-hundred-page manual and you still cannot ride; you can only *become* a body that rides. This is the kind of knowing the philosopher Polanyi called **tacit knowing** — and riding a bicycle is its classic example. The difference shows in how each can be undone. A memo can be thrown away — it is an object detachable from you. "Being able to ride" cannot be thrown away — it is no longer information but part of you, and losing it would just be becoming a different you. So the real axis is not *inside versus outside the body* but **detachable versus non-detachable.** And here sits one interesting twist. An entry on the chain is an explicit, external inscription — and yet it lives on the "becoming" side. **Because it cannot be detached.** A system with its ledger torn out is not the same system minus its records; it is a different system. A human's becoming happens tacitly (Polanyi); this system's becoming happens by explicit inscription. Opposite in form — identical in non-detachability.

One objection is worth answering before it is raised: "you say there is no outside arbiter, but doesn't this system *rely on* the blockchain machinery?" The answer is to split "rely on" into its two meanings. The chain is **not an arbiter** that vouches for the content of a change — the chain holds no opinion about whether a change is good or true. What the chain does is be the **place where governance events happen.** Just as the paper and ink of a marriage register are not a witness standing outside the marriage but the medium in which the marriage is constituted. A witness can fail to see; a medium cannot "fail to witness" what happens within it. (The honest caveat below applies here too: in the current implementation there is still a gap through which a change can happen and fail to reach the medium at all. The design's whole aim is to close exactly that gap, so that happening and reaching-the-medium are one step.) And depending on machinery as a substrate is admitted from the start — §4's "closed at governance, dependent at execution." Indeed the story runs the other way: it is *because* the governance loop closes on the inside that "becoming" is possible at all. If governance were administered by an outside control tower, a change would be something done *to* the system — information handed to it from outside. Only when the reorganisation is the system's own operation is it a *becoming*.

With that in place: the second design pillar is to separate two meanings hiding inside the word "record."

- **Evidential recording.** The event happens first; afterwards a log writes it down. The record is *about* the event. Delete it and you lose the audit trail, but the event still happened. A ship's logbook is this: the voyage proceeds whether or not the captain writes it up. This breaks by *forgetting to write* — because it is a rule (a policy).
- **Constitutive recording.** Here the act of recording *is* the event. A governance-level change takes effect **only by being recorded.** The entry carved into the append-only chain is the change's very mode of existence, not a description after the fact.

In one line: an ordinary log is a record of what the system **did**. A constitutive record is a record of what the system **is** — not because it explains what the system is, but because the inscribed sequence *is* the "what it is."

Two familiar things work this way. A **bank balance** is not a description of money kept somewhere else — the ledger entry *is* the balance. Change the ledger and you have changed the money. A **marriage registration** is the same — in systems like Japan's, where the marriage comes into being only by being registered: the marriage does not happen first and get recorded later; the signed notification being accepted into the register is what makes the marriage.

This distinction itself is not this note's invention. The philosopher Searle analysed **institutional facts** — facts sustained by everyone recognising them as such, with declaration and registration as their paradigm devices — and money and marriage are his canonical examples (the two examples above deliberately follow him). Law has long drawn the same line between *constitutive* registration, where the right exists only through the entry, and merely declaratory registration, which announces a right that already existed (the German land register is the textbook case). And in software, event sourcing comes close — the event log is the authoritative source, and state is derived from it — while blockchains implement it to the letter: a bitcoin balance exists nowhere but in its ledger entries. What this note adds is the target and the manner of enforcement: carrying the distinction into the **governance record of a system that changes its own rules**, and recasting it as the difference between guarding by rule (policy) and guarding by construction.

The phrase **"by construction"** captures the difference in one word — **the manner of enforcement.** A rule breaks if forgotten. A construction cannot break, because the recording step and the taking-effect step are, by design, **the same single step.** There is no admissible state of "the change happened but wasn't recorded," just as there is no "the money moved but the ledger didn't."

One question every practitioner will ask deserves an answer here. "Why not just manage the Skills — governance rules included — on GitHub?" Git has hash-linked history, signatures, review gates. The answer is not that Git is weak. **It sits on the wrong side of the distinction we just drew.** In Git, committing and taking effect are not coupled — the running system's effective rules are the files on disk, and what the repository holds is "whatever someone chose to commit": recording as discipline, the policy side exactly. The approval record, too, lives outside the hash-linked history — on a hosting company's pull-request page. And administering governance through an external platform is precisely the "outside control tower" that §4 declined — the change becomes something done *to* the system, handed to it rather than become. None of this is a defect of Git: Git was built to version content, not to constitute it. Put the other way around: anyone who wires a runtime to treat the repository as the sole source of effective rules, records approvals inside the history, and makes taking-effect and committing one single step has not refuted this design — **they have implemented it, with Git as the storage layer.** The claim is about a property, not a storage technology.

**This is the most honest part of the canonical, and the one point the plain version must never dilute.** What constitutive recording delivers is only **part** of this ideal. Three events make the borderline visible.

1. Rule A is adopted — an effective change.
2. A week later, rule A is quietly reverted — this too is an effective change.
3. Rule B is proposed, and voted down — nothing changed.

- The part that is delivered: **events 1 and 2 are guaranteed to be on the chain.** So no history can be presented in which "A was never there" — because the reversal (2) is itself an un-erasable record. The governance trajectory (which rules were adopted, amended, revoked) becomes fact rather than staged evidence. — But this promise carries a **double** limit. First, it **presupposes the operator's honest operation** (see the scope caveat below: a single operator can rewrite their own chain and recompute all the hashes; what stops them is not the chain alone but external anchoring). Second, this is a guarantee **"by design," and operation has not yet caught up with it.** In the current implementation, a small gap remains between writing a change to a file and recording it, and a crash in that gap can drop the record. "This is how it is by design" and "this actually holds on every code path" are different things, and the work of closing that distance is still going on, layer by layer. So the "cannot be hidden" of events 1 and 2 is read accurately as a **design goal the implementation is approaching**, not an achieved operational state.
- The part that is **not** delivered: **event 3 is outside the promise.** A rejected proposal changes nothing, so nothing obliges it onto the chain — and the same goes for failed runs and dead ends. This **outcome-independence** — recording failures and dead ends as much as successes — is **not achieved by construction anywhere in the present design, not even at the governance boundary.** §9 honestly leaves it as homework.

One more scope caveat. For a chain a single operator runs on their own machine, "irreversible" and "append-only" are guarantees **relative to that operator's honest operation.** A hash chain makes tampering **evident** to anyone who already holds a trusted copy of the chain's latest hash; it does **not** make it impossible for the operator themselves to rewrite history and recompute every hash forward. What keeps the operator honest is **external anchoring** — committing the chain's state into a record the operator does not control — and the Zenodo publication in §8 is exactly one such anchor.

And recording changes the **kind of time** the system lives in. A log lives in **Chronos** — clock-time, one interchangeable instant after another. Constitutive recording lives in **Kairos** — the Greek word for the decisive, non-interchangeable moment. Each carved entry is an irreversible reconstitution of what the system is. A boundary crossing (§3), once recorded, is no longer information the system *has*. It becomes part of what the system *is*.

Read §4 and §5 together and they come to one thing. §4 made the line between what the system governs and what it only runs on *visible*; §5 made every crossing of that line *recorded* — by construction at the governance layer, as far as §5's migration has carried that guarantee. An ordinary system leaves this boundary implicit and unlogged; a self-amending one built this way makes it explicit, and its crossings checkable from outside — the system still can't see its own ceiling from inside (§2 forbids that), but an outside observer can. That is the boundary §6 now puts a human on.

## 6. Put a human on the boundary

Why is a *watcher* not enough? The bridge of §5 continues here. What constitutive recording secures is the **form** — effective changes cannot be hidden. But the recording machinery cannot generate the **content**: *what is worth writing in.* If the system judged that alone, the judgment itself would be handed down from inside its own frame — **the blind spot grading itself** — and the ceiling (§2) does not allow that. So the judgment of what is worth writing in needs the act of someone with a *different* frame — a partial outside (§3) — to be involved. Not, however, at the same depth for every change. The deeper the layer, the heavier the involvement — until, at the deepest step of all, **rewriting the system's own constitution (L0)**, it becomes something that cannot be skipped.

This is the difference between "supervisor" and "standing on the boundary." A supervisor belongs to the evidential vocabulary — watching, commenting afterwards, ignorable; it breaks the same way "forgetting to write" does. **Even "gatekeeper" is still the evidential picture** — a gatekeeper stands outside the gate, inspecting what passes, and can doze off. What happens here is different. Standing on the boundary belongs to the constitutive vocabulary — at the constitution-rewriting step, on top of taking-effect and recording being one single step (§5), **the human approval is built in as a component of that step.** When the approval is absent, the gate is not closed; **the gate does not exist.** So at that step, and that step only, there is no admissible state of "it took effect without the approval." You could say it is **constitutive recording extended to the human act.** (At shallower layers — updating reusable knowledge (L1), for example — a change can be recorded on the chain and take effect without human approval. The gate is constitutively built in at the constitutional step only.)

If a concrete picture helps, take a bank's **safe-deposit box**. The door has two keyholes: it opens only when the clerk's key and the customer's key are turned together. Now compare that clerk with the security guard sitting in front of the camera monitors. The guard is a "supervisor" — watching, able to report afterwards. But he can doze off, and the door opens whether or not he is there. The two-key clerk is different. Unless he turns his key, the event "the door opens" **does not exist at all.** He is not watching over the event; he is **a component of it.** The human approval at the constitutional step is this **second key.**

So the human is not a "supervisor in the loop." The human stands **on the boundary.** The human's acts — approving a change, objecting to it, reframing it — are the events the system actually passes through when it redefines itself. The system's boundary is, in part, **made of** those acts.

It is worth saying precisely what comes into being here. Not an enlarged human frame, and not an enlarged LLM frame — neither party gets out of its own frame (§3). What comes into being is a **third thing**: the composite system of human × LLM × record, which has its own, new outermost frame. The frames did not fuse into a higher ceiling; **one more inside came into existence** — and that inside has a ceiling of its own. This is exactly what §9 will put last — "any mechanism that takes the outside in creates a new inside" — and it is an engine, not a flaw.

And standing on the boundary changes *you* as well. The crossings of §3 ran both ways — the LLM reaches your blind side too. When a crossing lands in your blind spot and you take it in, what happens is not the addition of information but a **reorganisation of how you see.** In §5's terms, it is *your* side of "becoming" — though a human's becoming, like the bicycle, happens tacitly. Standing at the boundary, again and again, is itself the practice by which the shape of your cognition changes. To build your acts into the composite system is **at the same time to remake yourself** — and this is exactly what the next section will call the operator's own transformation. The chain holds the **system's half** of that joint work, in a form that can be shown to a third party. The record does not become part of you directly; but it carves the trace your acts passed through — **the half inside you and the half on the chain are two faces of the same sequence of crossings.**

An insight from outside (a reviewer's finding, a reframing the model could never have reached from inside its own frame) enters first as throwaway working context (L2), is distilled into reusable knowledge (L1) if it proves its worth, and may eventually be written into the system's own constitution (L0). Each promotion is recorded on the chain, and the step that writes into the constitution (L1→L0) passes through a **human gate.** What the ceiling forbids as a "one-time heroic act" — self-transcendence — is not even *approached* (transcendence has no "halfway outside"). It is **functionally substituted**: a repeated, recorded, two-party exchange delivers part of what transcendence would have delivered, without anyone ever standing in a transcendent place.

## 7. Experience as capital

§5 ended on a big claim: what the system *is*, is its carved history. This section draws the economic face of that claim. If the system's being is its recorded trajectory, then so is its **value** — the asset is not the copyable machinery, and not even the record as a lump of bits, but the anchored trajectory of boundary crossings (§3). That is the sense in which this design turns accumulated **experience into capital.** Accountants have long had a name for the value a going business carries beyond its listed assets: **goodwill** — reputation, relationships, know-how. Goodwill is asserted and estimated; nobody can audit it. What constitutive recording builds is, so to speak, **goodwill you can audit.**

This must be said carefully. A hash chain, being a file, can be copied bit for bit. What the construction guarantees is not that the trajectory **cannot be copied** but that a copy **cannot be a continuation.** Someone who copies the mechanism and the chain whole gets not a clone of you but a **fork.**

This is exactly the situation of a forked code repository. A fork copies the entire change-history perfectly. What cannot be copied is **which fork the community, the maintainers, and the signed releases go on recognising as the line of descent.** The bits are shared; the lineage is attributed. At the instant of copying, the two constitutions are identical (the bits are the same). Originality is not a property of the byte sequence but a property of **the subsequent, anchored trajectory.** The moment each side begins recording its own boundary crossings, the two constitutions diverge, and "which one is the original?" becomes **a matter of audit, not of assertion** — settled not by the chain alone but by the external anchors of §5 (which pin a trajectory to a public identity and public times).

And the other half of the asset cannot be moved at all. The **operator's own transformation** — the years of reframings a human gave and received; the accumulation of what §6 ended with, boundary work remaking the person — is tacit experience — the same kind of knowing as §5's "being able to ride" (Polanyi) — and no recording technology in history has ever copied tacit experience. What constitutive recording adds is the **system's half**: it makes the system's side of the shared history inspectable, attributable, and divergence-evident. So the experience the pair accumulated is not merely real but **demonstrable** to a third party.

And this settles the practical worry such a design always provokes: "if I publish the design, won't I hand over my advantage?" Under evidential recording, maybe — there the mechanism is the asset, so copying the mechanism copies the asset, and publishing is giving it away. Here the value never lived in the mechanism, so publishing hands over nothing that was not already fork-evident (the system's half) or uncopyable (yours). In a future where frontier models (the most capable models) are rentable by anyone, what distinguishes one human×agent pair from another is exactly this — **the recorded history of their collaboration at the boundary.**

## 8. Embed the canonical in the system it describes (not this plain version)

It would be inconsistent for a document that argues recording is constitutive to settle for being merely evidential about itself. So the **canonical (the English technical note)** is written into the very system it describes — not as an outside note *about* a contribution, but as an **L1 reference Skill entered into the system's own knowledge and recorded on its chain**, so the document becomes part of what the system *is* (the procedure is canonical §8, carried out at the published version). **To be clear —** what is deposited on Zenodo, given a DOI, and recorded on the chain is the **canonical**, not this plain version you are reading now (this plain version is for author / transmission use and is not published). The steps are:

- **Freeze and hash the raw file.** What gets deposited is a single frozen file (the English source, LF + Unicode NFC, nothing else); the fingerprint is just the SHA-256 of its raw bytes — no field to blank, no surgery. Into it go only three fixed **names** (not fingerprints): the reserved Zenodo DOI, a direct link to the note's source file in the repository, and a stable **verification address**.
- **Commit it constitutively.** Record that hash on the system's own chain — the entry by which the document, held as an L1 reference Skill, becomes part of what the system is. The record points *from* the chain *to* the document (via the verification address); the document gets no record number back and carries none.
- **Publish and demonstrate.** Deposit the frozen file on Zenodo under the reserved DOI — the **public reference**, and (a record the operator does not control) the external anchor of §5. On the shared Meeting Place, register only the matching **attestation** (a digest pointing at the Zenodo and repository copies) — the body stays on Zenodo. That is the **place where the constitutive recording is demonstrated**. For your own chain it is a public reference, not a further anchor; for someone else's instance the same attestation *is* a real external anchor — so the role is relational.

Why does this not chase its own tail? The document is recorded, and the record refers to the document — but the reference runs **one way only**, from the record to the document, never from the document to a fingerprint of itself. The document carries no hash of itself and no record number — only the three fixed names, set before hashing. So a reader just downloads the file from Zenodo, takes its SHA-256, and matches it against the hash in the attestation — recomputing the fingerprint independently, which is what makes the check meaningful rather than circular. The loop that remains is the intended one: a document about constitutive recording, constitutively recorded. It lives in the *act of recording*, not in any number the document prints — which is why the document need carry nothing of itself.

One more thing the canonical's §8 honestly admits, carried over here. What this embedding demonstrates is **constitutivity**, not outcome-independence. What gets published is the surviving draft of the canonical, and the history that led there (the reversals successive review rounds left behind, from v0.1 onward) is exactly the selectively-remembered material §5 assigns to the disposable layer. So the very document that argues this distinction is itself an instance of the **weaker** side of it — a constitutively-fixed conclusion drawn from a selectively-remembered process. This is not a contradiction to hide; it is the same asymmetry the system lives under, traced once more by the document about the system.

## 9. What is not yet done (honestly)

- This is a design note. It makes no new empirical claim. The ceiling (§2) is **argued** in the strange-loop lineage, not **measured** — and the difference matters. To *measure* it, you would gather many game transcripts and point: "there, that's the boundary." But since any record is made from the inside, no transcript showing the *outside* of the ceiling can exist. To *argue* it, instead, you follow a single move to its logical end — as with §1's opening proposal (rejecting "the game begins from player A" itself needs a vote, and that vote presupposes the game has already begun), which shows why a reach toward the outside necessarily lands back inside. So the ceiling is not something you confirm by measuring, but something you grant by following the argument.
- The human in the boundary role is frame-bound too (the symmetry of §3). We do not claim the human supplies a "view from nowhere" — only a "view from **somewhere else**."
- Selective attestation (the middle instrument that adds opt-in tamper-detection to chosen units of work) is not outcome-independence. Because a human chooses *what* to record, the **absence** of a record there proves nothing. And a deeper limit: full outcome-independence is not delivered by construction **anywhere** today, not even at the governance boundary. Where the line should fall — which acts must be recorded regardless of outcome, and which may be left to human choice — is not yet drawn.
- Any mechanism that "takes the outside in" creates a new inside, whose outside must again be supplied from without. This is not a flaw but an **engine.** Precisely because complete self-description is unavailable in principle, the system's evolution is **driven** by the residue of what it cannot yet describe about itself.
- The argument has been stated for **one concrete system**, KairosChain, because constitutive recording is an architectural property a system either has or lacks — it cannot be claimed in the abstract. The design pattern, though — meta and base written in the same structure, construction-level recording, an institutionalised boundary — is offered for anyone to reuse (§7 is the reason reuse does not dilute the original).

## 10. Why not aim for "everyone understands it"

Finally, a word on where this plain version itself stands.

This document is not for making **everyone** understand the canonical. The canonical's density (argued not measured) is its virtue, and flatten it and something always dies. So the goal here is **partial resonance** — the stance that it is enough if one reader receives one corner deeply.

And this lines up exactly with the paper's own claim. As §3 says, value comes not from "the other internalising all of you" but from "one corner landing deeply in the other's territory and sending back an application you did not anticipate." **A partial outside is enough.** Whether it is the one example of the bank ledger, or the two drivers' blind spots, or the lineage of a git fork — if any one of them lands deeply in you, this plain version has finished its work.
